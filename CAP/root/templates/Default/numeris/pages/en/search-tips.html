Search Tool with Examples

Introduction

The search tool lets you do the following:

  • Search documents by the document title, subject keywords, or their text content.
  • Modify or refine search results in a number of ways.
  • Open a document and browse its content.

The Start Screen, in Figure 1 below, lets you start a search by entering information in any of the areas on the right. In these areas you can do the following:

  • Type text to be searched in the SEARCH box.

  • Indicate where the text will be searched in a document. There are two options:

    • SIMPLE SEARCH: From the ‘Search in’ drop-down list, select an area in the documents (i.e. Everything, Titles, Subjects or Text) where all the terms in the SEARCH box will be searched.

    • ADVANCED SEARCH: Add the prefix ti:, su: or tx: to a term in the SEARCH box to request that the term be searched in the title, subject or text, respectively. You may add different prefixes to different terms (e.g. ti:television su:Halifax will search the term "television" on titles and the term "Halifax" on subjects). More about prefixes below.

  • Narrow the search to a year range (Date Range boxes).

The Results Screen, in Figure 2 below, allows you to view the results list, open a document, modify or refine the results of a search, or start a new search, as explained in the examples below.

The Viewer Screen, in Figure 4 below, allows you to view a document, browse its pages and search its text content, as explained below.


Figure 1. Start Screen

The examples below use wildcards and prefixes in the text search. To learn about the use of conditionals (and, or) and wildcards (*, ?), how to enter terms to search for phrases, or how to search multiple fields at once (use of ti:, su:), see section Search Tool Tips below. For search tips specific to the collection, see section Collection-Specific Search Tips below.

Example 1 – Start a Search

Assume you want to find audience data on the television show M.A.S.H. in the 1980s. In the Start Screen in Figure 1 above, type "M.A.S.H." (quotes included, otherwise periods would be ignored) in the SEARCH box, select Everything from the drop-down list, type 1980 and 1989 in the Date Range boxes, and click Search. This will list all the documents related to a survey conducted between 1980 and 1989 (both years included) that contain the term "M.A.S.H." anywhere in their content (including titles and subjects, if any). The Results Screen will look like Figure 2 below, with the list of results on the right pane and options for refining the search on the left. Pages ("images") where the term has been found are listed below the title.


Figure 2. Results Screen

From this screen you can do any of the following:

On the right:

  • Click a title (or an image link) to open the item –more on how to search within an open document in Example 3 below.
  • Display information on the document (e.g. title, alternative title, subject, description/notes) by clicking the Information button (+) to the right of the title.

On the left:

  • Sort the results by relevance, newest or oldest.
  • Modify or refine your search criteria (see Example 3 below):
    • Type or modify text (SEARCH box)
    • Select where to search (option in ‘Search in’ drop-down list or prefixes su:, ti: in SEARCH box).
    • Remove or change years date range (Date Range boxes).
    • Filter by Language (click English or French).
  • Update your search after modifying entry fields above (Update button).
  • Review help information (Help button).

Example 2 – Modify a Search to Refine Results

Starting with the results of the first search pictured above (Figure 2), which lists the documents that contain the term "M.A.S.H." (anywhere in the document) for surveys between 1980 and 1989 (both years included), assume you want to find audience data of the final episode of the M.A.S.H. television series. After a quick online search, you find it was broadcast on February 28, 1983. In the form, modify the Date Range to 1983 in both boxes, and modify the SEARCH box to contain "M.A.S.H." Toronto, and click Update.

NOTE: When the Update button is clicked, a new search is executed according to the SEARCH box content and other filters. If you delete "M.A.S.H." and add Toronto, all documents with the word "Toronto" in their content will be found (i.e. "M.A.S.H." was no longer a search term).

After browsing the results (Figure 2), you notice books for other markets, such as Brandon and Vancouver, not only Toronto. This is because the word "Toronto" appears as part of the address of Numeris’s Head Office on the back cover of all the books printed in 1983. To eliminate other markets, modify the search text to contain "M.A.S.H." su:Toronto and click Update. Adding the prefix "su:" restricts the search of term "Toronto" to the Subject field (see note below regarding markets in subjects vs. titles). Some results are pictured in Figure 3 below.

NOTE: The collection includes Regional and Market books. Regional books, such as an Ontario book, were structured to contain reports on a number of markets not listed in their title. The Subject field has been structured to contain, among other pieces of information, the list of markets. Both Title and subject fields of individual Market books, such as a Toronto book, contain the market. In the current example, restricting the search to either ti:Toronto or su:Toronto retrieves the same Market books for the year 1983, when individual books were printed. As a general rule, when searching books related to a particular market, searching the subject may yield more accurate results. For markets or areas that may be too small to be included as a market, searching the text content may be the best approach.


Figure 3. Refining search results

From the results, notice that there are books for different surveys in 1983: Spring, Summer, Winter and Fall. To find out whether February 28, the final episode of the series, was included in one of the surveys, you can do any of the following:

  • Open a title to see the dates on the cover (note that some books may not have dates on the cover, but inside the book).
  • Display information on the document by clicking the Information button (+) to the right of the title, and look at the dates in the Notes/Description field.
  • Look up the survey dates in the section Diary Surveys to be certain.

You find out the date was included in the Spring survey, so you click on the title link to open the book and search through the pages.

Example 3 – Search Text and Browse a Document in the Viewer

Figure 4 below shows the open television book for the markets Toronto and Hamilton from the 1983 Spring survey in the Viewer Screen. From the previous example on Figure 3 above, we see that this book was one of the results of searching "M.A.S.H." su:Toronto, for documents related to surveys from 1983; therefore, the pages listed contains the term "M.A.S.H.".

In the Viewer you can do the following:

  1. Go back to search results.
  2. Search text within the document (Search box/Search button).
  3. Go to a page where the text on the Search box is found by clicking an image link (below the Search box).
  4. Switch between the document view and information view by clicking the View Tab and About Tab (below the document title).
  5. Go to a page by selecting the "image" from the drop-down list on the left of the Tools Bar located above the document image.
  6. Navigate to the first/next/previous/last page (Tools Bar).
  7. Rotate a page (Tools Bar).
  8. Zoom a page (Tools Bar).
  9. Download the document (Tools Bar).
  10. Go to the next and previous page by clicking the gray area near the left and right of the image.


Figure 4. Viewer Screen

Continuing with the example, you can locate the audience for the "M.A.S.H." finale in the Program section of the book (Image 50).

While the book is open, you look for information on the show "Diff’rent Strokes" (actual title spelling) in March 19, 1983 (date covered by the same Spring survey), when US First Lady Nancy Reagan made a special appearance on the show, according to online sources. You may not know how the program title was printed in the book. You try different searches, such as "diff'rent strokes", "different strokes" or diff*nt strok*. The first two approaches search for the exact phase in the document. The last approach, which includes the "*" wildcard, searches for both partially matched terms occurring in the same page when searching within a book (see Example 4 below for more details). This matches titles such as "different strokes", "different stroke", "diff’rent strokes" and "diff’rent stroke". In this particular book, the program was printed as "DIFFERENT STROKE" (without ending "S"); however, some books for other years have the title as "DIFF’RENT STROKE".

Example 4 – Search Two or More Terms in Text Content

There is a caveat when searching two or more terms in the text content of books, depending on whether you search in the Start/Results Screen or in the Viewer screen (an open book). Below is a quick summary of the differences, followed by an example.

  • In the Start or Results screen: A search of two or more terms separated by spaces (the "and" operator) finds all the books where both terms are found in any of the pages, not necessarily within the same page. However, only pages where both terms are found within the page are listed under the title. When a book is found where the terms don’t appear in the same page, a "No matching pages found" label is displayed instead of a page list under the title.

  • In the Viewer screen (open book): A search of two or more terms separated by spaces (the "and" operator) in the Viewer finds all the pages where both terms are found within the page. These pages are listed under the title. To look for either of the words within the book, the "|" operator (the "or" operator) must be included between the terms.

Example
You want to start a search for books with the TV show "Diff’rent Strokes". You may not know how the program title was printed in the book, so you try diff*nt strok*, which will search for both terms partially matched by the wildcard. Figure 5 below shows the results.


Figure 5. Results of searching two terms.

After sorting by "oldest", you notice two things in the results. First, titles from 1971, when the show started in 1978. And second, "No matching pages found" under some titles. As explained above, the books found contain both terms, although not necessarily in the same page. The books with "No matching pages found" are the books with no instances of both terms in the same page (i.e., the terms appear in different pages). You open the North Bay book, by example, and adjust the search text to diff*nt | strok* (with the "or" operator) to obtain the list of pages where one or the other term is matched, as pictured in Figure 6 below. A closer inspection of the pages found (pages not shown) reveals several instances of the word "different" in the introductory text and one instance of "stroke" as a program title.


Figure 6. Search within a document.

Example 5 – Use of Knowledge of Document Content and Metadata Structure to Guide a Search

Depending on the information you want to find, you may use different approaches to guide your search. For example, rather than simply entering a radio call letter into the SEARCH box, narrowing the search based on other known information may be a better approach. Useful information includes the type of content in different reports available and the call letter used in the reports to identify the station. Be aware:

  • Call letters can change over time
  • The same call letters can be used by different stations at different times
  • Numeris uses the same call letters as the C.R.T.C. to identify radio stations and television local (conventional) stations in reports
  • Numeris may create short forms for certain call letters

As an example of narrowing results, assume you want to know the morning audience of radio station CAAA (not a real call letter) in Edmonton in the fall of 1995. You know that audience at particular times of the day (time blocks) may be found in the station report section of a Radio Market book. After you enter su:radio su:Edmonton su:market su:fall su:1995 in the SEARCH box (i.e. each term as part of the Subject field), you locate the Edmonton Radio Market book for Fall 1995. You open the book and search for the call letter, either by entering the call letter in the SEARCH box or by scrolling through the pages to find the station report section.

NOTE: Scroll through the pages as you would with a paper book when unsure about findings; do not rely on text search only. Optical character recognition, or OCR, is not 100% accurate, and may not correctly identify every instance of a call letter.

Searchable Information

Following is a summary of the information that can be searched to locate or filter items in the collection. Knowing the structure of these fields may help in formulating a search.

Title (prefix: ti:)

  • Titles are in both English and French.
  • Titles are typically structured to include the market name (for individual Market Reports) or the region name (for Regional Reports), the media (e.g. radio or television), and the survey and year.
  • Additional information may be included, such as whether the item is a revised version ("Revised") or a special report ("Special").
  • A market list is not included in the titles of Regional Reports (e.g. an Atlantic Report). The markets are included in the Subject field (see below).

Examples:

  • Calgary - Television Market Report/Rapport de marché - Fall/Automne 2000
  • Atlantic - Radio Market Report/Rapport de marché - Spring/Printemps 2002
  • Radio Reach/Portée - Spring/Printemps 1985

Subject (prefix: su:)

Subject text is in both English and French, and is structured as follows:

Media -- Report type -- Survey and year -- Market(s)

where each element may have one (or more, separated by commas) of the following values:

  • Media:
    • radio
    • television TV télévision
  • Report type:
    • market report/rapport de marché (includes both Market Reports and Regional Reports)
    • reach report/rapport de portée
    • network report/rapport de réseaux
    • special report/rapport spécial
  • Survey and year:
    • Fall yyyy/Automne yyyy
    • Spring yyyy/Printemps yyyy
    • Summer yyyy /Été yyyy
    • Winter yyyy/Hiver yyyy
  • Market(s) : (may not apply to some Report Types)
    • Toronto
    • Québec
    • Vancouver
    • Calgary
    • Edmonton
    • etc.

Examples (corresponding to the examples above):

  • television TV télévision -- market report/rapport de marché -- Fall 2000/Automne 2000 -- Calgary
  • radio -- market report/rapport de marché -- Spring 2002/Printemps 2002 -- Moncton, Saint John, St. John's, Halifax
  • radio -- reach report/rapport de portée -- Spring 1985/Printemps 1985 -- n/a

Markets are not included in the Subject field of "Reach" Reports. Use Text content search instead.

Text Content

Text in pages, in the document’s original language.

Notes/Description

Includes additional information on the survey, typically dates, in both English and French. Field only searchable when searching "Everything" (no prefix).

Examples (corresponding to the examples above):

  • Fall/Automne 2000: Oct/oct. 19-25, Nov/nov. 2-8, Nov/nov. 9-15, Nov/nov. 16-22
  • Spring/Printemps 2002: Feb/févr. 18 - Apr/avril 14
  • Spring/Printemps 1985: Apr/avril 22-28, Apr/avril 29 - May/mai 5, May/mai 6-12

Survey Year

The survey year associated with a document is searched by the tool when a year range is provided to narrow a search.

Language

"English", "French" or both have been assigned to every item in the collection, depending on the item’s content language. This field is used to narrow the results. Most of the items in the collection are bilingual (Markets and Regional Reports). Separate English and French versions of some Reference Material may be available.

Search Tool Tips

Tips in this section focus on how text entered in the SEARCH box is handled by the search tool, and are not specific to the collection. For search tips specific to the collection, see Collection-Specific Search Tips below.

The search form is a simple tool that lets you search by title, subject keywords, or the text content documents. The following features can be used within the SEARCH box to search or refine the results:

Search terms are not case or accent-sensitive

For example, "Montréal" and "montreal" will produce the same result. However, the prefixes ti: and su: (see below) must be in lower case.

Wildcards: ? and *

Use ? in the middle or at the end of a word to match any one character. Use * to match any number of characters (including zero).

  • Example: to find either catherine or catharine: cath?rine
  • Example: to find either labour or labor: labo*r
  • Not allowed inside a phrase (i.e. inside double quotes).

Phrases: ""

To search for an exact phrase, enclose all of the words in double quotes.

  • Example: to find documents that contain the phrase montreal anglophone and not just the individual terms montreal and anglophone: "montreal anglophone"
  • You cannot use wildcards within a phrase.
  • Characters such as , . - / are ignored.

All terms ("and" operator): space

To match all the terms, separate them by spaces.

  • Example: to find toronto and hamilton : toronto Hamilton
  • See below regarding searching 2 or more terms in the Start/Result screen vs. in the Viewer.

Alternate terms ("or" operator): |

To match any one of a number of alternative terms, put a | between them.

  • Example: to find ottawa and either hull or gatineau : ottawa hull | gatineau

Excluded terms: -

Use the character " - " in front of a word or phrase to indicate that the term must not appear in the record.

  • Example, to find documents that have radio and reach in the title but not county (see below about prefix ti:): ti:radio ti:reach -ti:county

Field search: ti:, su:

Use ti: or su: in front of a word or phrase to search only document titles or document subjects.

  • Unlike text searched, prefixes are case sensitive. Use lower case only.
  • Example: to find documents that contain Toronto in the title: ti:Toronto
  • Example: to find documents that contain Barrie in the subject: su:Barrie
  • Example: to find documents that contain television and reach in the title: ti:television ti:reach

Searching two or more terms with the "and" operator (space)

  • In the Start or Results screen: A search of two or more terms separated by spaces (the "and" operator) in the Start/Results Screen will find all the documents where both terms are found in any of the pages, not necessarily within the same page. However, only pages where both terms are found within the page are listed under the title. When a document is found where the terms don’t appear in the same page, a "No matching pages found" label is displayed instead of a page list under the title.

  • In the Viewer (open document): A search of two or more terms separated by a spaces (the "and" operator) in the Viewer will find all the pages where both terms are found within the page. These pages are listed under the title. To look for either of the terms within the document, use the "|" operator between the terms.

Collection-Specific Search Tips

This section presents simple tips to help locate a variety of content in the collection based on collection features, such as type of material (e.g. Reach Report), material content and metadata structure (e.g. information included in Subject field.

As mentioned in other sections, the search tool lets you search by document title, subject keywords, or the text content of documents.

The Subject field is structured to contain the following information: media, report type, survey name and year, and markets; for more information, see section Searchable Information above.

Radio and Television

Tip # 1: Restrict search to the Radio or Television collection

Search the media in the Subject field with the "su:" prefix. Example: su:television or su:radio.

Tip # 2: Locate documents related to a specific market

To locate Market or Regional Reports related to a market, search the market in the Subject field**. Market search by subject provides better results than searching for a market as part of the title. You may specify the report type, which is also included in the Subject field. Example: su:moncton su:"market report".

To locate Reach Reports that include a market, search the market in the text content and restrict the report type in the subject. Example: moncton su:reach. Reach Reports do not list the markets in the Subject field.

Small areas not considered markets, and therefore not included in the Subject field, may be found by searching the text content.

** Currently, Regional Reports do not list the related markets in the Subject field. The field will be updated with the market list in the future.

Tip # 3: Locate documents related to a specific survey and year

Search the survey name and year in the Subject field separately with the "su:" prefix. Example: su:fall su:1981. Alternately, you may enter the year in the Date Range boxes.

Tip # 4: Find a call letter

Rather than simply entering a call letter into the SEARCH box, narrowing the search based on other known information may be a better approach. Helpful information includes the type of content of the different reports available (e.g. su:market for Market or Regional Reports) and the call letter used in the reports to identify the station. Be aware:

  • Call letters can change over time
  • The same call letters can be used by different stations at different times
  • Numeris uses the same call letters as the C.R.T.C. to identify radio stations and television local (conventional) stations in reports
  • Numeris may create short forms for certain call letters

Scroll through the pages as you would with a paper book when unsure about findings; do not rely on text search only. Optical character recognition, or OCR, is not 100% accurate, and may not correctly identify every instance of a call letter.

Television

Tip #5: Search a television program title

Program titles may be abbreviated due to space limitations. You may try using the "*" wildcard. Keep in mind that wildcards do not work within a phrase (double-quotes).

Titles can be abbreviated a number of ways. If you are not successful locating a title, try scrolling through the program section of a report looking for the title.
